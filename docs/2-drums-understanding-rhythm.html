2 - Drums: Understanding rhythm

10/27/2024 Categories: Music Theory

Note: This post will be longer than usual due to all the information needed to get started. Sorry!

In this chapter, we will learn about rhythm and duration in music by writing the drum loop of our track. But first, there are a few things that you should be aware of.

Before getting started

First, don't be too harsh on yourself. Each topic that I will cover is an entire subject by itself, with a lot to learn, and much practice needed to be comfortable with their concepts. If you are lost, it is OK! Take it as an introductory course, an overview. Don't hesitate to ask questions! If you plan to dive deeper into writing music, this will give you some insight into what resources and topics to look for.

Secondly, the order of topics is arbitrary. All musicians have their own preferences when it come to writing music. Here, I tried to order things so that the learning process is as smooth as possible. If you try the whole process again later by yourself, use whatever order you wish!

Lastly, we are going to use a piece of software called a DAW, which stands for Digital Audio Workstation (read more on Wikipedia). Basically, it is a virtual studio to create and process audio files and streams.
The DAW that we will use in this course is BandLab. It is free, available in a browser or as a web app, and beginner friendly. However, you can quickly get limited, and it is unstable with a bad internet connection (I lost the draft of this course that way, it refused to save!). So, I recommend creating "duplicates" frequently, saving your regions or patterns to MIDI and taking screenshots of your effects configuration. Better be safe than sorry!
Alternatively, free DAWs include LMMS and Ardour. You can also use a trial of some professional software, such as Ableton Live (90 days), Bitwig (30 days, too short!), FL Studio (cannot open saves in the trial), etc. There are also mobile apps of varying quality, which I would generally not recommend. Other than that, use what you want!

OK, that being said, let's dive right into it!

Time measurement: beat, bars, bpm

In music, a unit of time is a beat. We group beats into bars. Bars are used to group beats and to ease areas delimitation. For instance, we use it to define the length of a chorus, a verse, or a bridge. We also usually change of chord on a bar change. If all of this is blurry, it is OK, just remember that commonly:

1 bar = 4 beats

Note: Some genres like waltz traditionally do not follow this rule. If you want to dive more into this concept, browse time signatures.

All note durations are measured in beats (although their names refer to duration based on bars). Here are the most common ones, represented in a music sheet and a MIDI grid (both represent the same melody).

OK, now that we share the same vocabulary, let's write our first drum loop!

A basic drum loop

If you haven't created your project at this point, create a new one and choose to start with a virtual instrument. You can also start from the MIDI example if you wish, in this case, click on the button with a "+" in the top left to add a virtual instrument track.

Notice that the instrument tab is now selected at the bottom. There, above the piano, choose a preset in the drum kits or drum pads. I will personally try to write some lo-fi music, so I will choose "Lofi Hop". I will also change the bpm at the top to 90 as I want it to be calm.

The most basic drum kit pattern uses three different percussions (a schema is available on Wikipedia): the kick or bass drum, the snare drum or just snare, and the hi-hat, which can be opened or closed. The kick is a deep heartbeat like sound, we will use it every beat (or quarter, 1 beat) as a reference. The snare is a powerful drum, we will use it to accentuate the second and fourth beat of each bar (using half notes, 2 beats). Finally, the hi-hat is useful for fast rhythmic. We will use eighth notes (1/2 beat), with one every four notes being an open hi-hat.

Double click at the start of the drum kit track (highlighted in purple on the picture above) to create a new region. Place your mouse at its end and drag the border to extend it to 4 bars instead of 1.

Let's move on to the MIDI Editor at the bottom left. Pick the pencil tool on the left to place notes with your mouse. Use the arrow to select notes by clicking or dragging. This allows you to cut, copy, paste, or delete notes. Use the magnifying glass icons to zoom the grid in or out. To increase the height of the grid, place your mouse on the top border of the panel and drag the mouse while having a double arrow cursor visible. DO NOT FORGET TO SAVE YOUR WORK REGULARLY!

Variation: spicing things up (OPTIONAL)

This sounds nice, but it is a bit boring and repetitive. If you have a bit of experience in music (or if you are curious), we can push things a bit further.

The first change that we are going to do is not that commonly heard, but is used in genres like blues or jazz, and by artists like TheFatRat. I am talking about the groove. Put simply, we extend then shrink alternatively the length of an eighth (1/2 beat). Usually, the beat is split as 2/3 and 1/3 instead of the common 1/2 and 1/2 split.

Right click on the MIDI grid, and choose 1/8T (which stands for 1/8 of a bar (= 1/2 beat), ternary mode). Notice that each beat now has three columns instead of two. Let's extend the first half of each beat to two cells, and the second half to one.

Tip: You can do this on a few beats and copy it fast. Go to mouse mode, select all the notes, hold Alt and the left click on a selected note, and move your mouse to the right. Do it as many time as needed, and don't hesitate to increase the selection to be even faster!

Now, let's change the pattern a bit. There is no rule here, it is mostly trying things and keeping what works out! However, you can get inspiration by carefully listening to your favourite music and paying attention to the drum pattern. It is difficult at first, but it is a great exercise and a great source of inspiration!

Here are my changes:

  • To mark the bar change a bit more, I add a kick at the end of the first and third bar.
  • To enlighten the middle of the pattern, I create a slightly unexpected variation with the hi-hat.
  • I lower the velocity (intensity and volume of a note) of some closed hi-hats coming before a beat to accentuate those beats. Not too much, we need to hear it! Around 70% is good. To do so, click on the V tool, and drag your mouse up and down on a note to change its velocity. You can also see the value and change it with the line below the velocity tool button, at the left of the panel.
  • I mark the end of the loop with toms (percussions of varying pitch but similar sound). Note that I remove the snare and hi-hat, because a real drummer (almost) cannot play both with their hands at the same time! I also use two triplets, a group of notes where each lasts 1/3 beat. I also put a ride cymbal in place of the hi-hat at the start as a checkpoint for the start of the loop. This whole pattern is extremely common in pop rock music.
