Today, we are going to talk about the Nemesis of song writers: turning a loop into a song (which could be a new year resolution!). Not because it is complicated, but because it can be time consuming, and most importantly requires inspiration. Which is one of the reasons why most of what musicians produce never leave their studio, by the way. So let's try to, hopefully, turn our loop from last chapter into a full track!

The different parts of a song

In popular and radio music, there are a few distinctive parts that are regularly put together in predefined patterns. And they all play a specific role in the song:

  • The intro: it sets the mood of the track, introduce the player to the ambiance and dynamics of it. It is also what leaves the first impression, and so should not be neglected!
  • The chorus: it is usually the most memorable part, the one everyone will sing along. It is repeated multiple times as a point of reference for the listener, and if lyrics are included, it reflects the core of the story.
  • The verse: it is what tells the story and where variation will usually lie, if any. It is common that the melody and chord progression stay almost the same, but the lyrics change. Eventually, the melody is adapted to fit the lyrics.
  • The bridge: it breaks the monotony of the song and can serve either as an interlude, or as a transition to another phase of the song. You will commonly find improvised solos in this part (such as from a guitar, a piano, etc.).
  • The outro: it closes the story, either abruptly or softly. It sometimes even is omitted if the song stops out of nowhere.

A track using the radio format will usually last around 3'30", which as usual is not an absolute rule, but rather an average. One of the most common patterns is as follows:

Intro - Verse - Chorus - Verse - Chorus - Bridge - Chorus - Outro

It is up to you to decide if your loop will be the chorus, the verse, etc. depending of how you are inspired. But that's where it gets hard, because you have to write all those parts, and they have to fit together! I have done it to showcase it but you are not obligated to do it, the rest of the chapters can be done with only a loop. Or you can do some parts, as you wish (that is, if you are actually following along of course).

There is no order in the whole process of writing all of them, it is all very organic, based on your inspiration, and that include transitions (which I'll explain right after).

The only thing to note when you start thinking about chaining them together is the attention span of the listener. If it gets too repetitive, they might skip the music. You also want to balance the overall energy so that the drop doesn't sound bland and disappointing.

Transitions

While you want to try to make the different parts go well together, usually the transition between two parts will be quite abrupt. That's where (actual) transitions come in. Their goal is to tease or smoothen the passing from one part to another. It can be simple, such as a noise sweep up or sweep down, an entire pre-drop part full of automation in EDM music, a simple reversed cymbal, etc. It can even be a silence!

Embellishment

Towards the end, you can also add details such as an ambiance background in the intro, sound effects or isolated short instrument tracks along the parts to spice things up a bit more, and reduce repetition. There is no science here, just experiment!
