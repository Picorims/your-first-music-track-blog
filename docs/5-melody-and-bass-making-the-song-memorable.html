5 - Melody and Bass: Making the Song Memorable

12/01/2024 Categories: Music Theory

Dev env info:

In the last chapter, we improved our chord progression to make it sound more interesting. Today is the time to give it a story!

The melody

A melody tells a story, even without lyrics, and even if it is just emotions. It is also what the listener remembers and sing along, so it plays an important role in your song.

Melody is all about experimenting until we find something that sounds nice (a lot of what musicians write never leave their studio!). But it can be hard for beginners especially if you never played an instrument.

Thankfully, we can use a simple scale that all instrument players know for improvising: the pentatonic scale. In our C Major scale (hint: school), the pentatonic (major) scale are the notes C, D, E, G and A (the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 5th and 6th notes of the scale). You know what is nice with these five notes? Whatever you play, it always sounds good! Try it out if you don't trust me.

Okay, that's cool, but how do we use them? Well, here are some tips to guide you:

  1. Use patterns (rhythm and/or notes) that you repeat to make it easier to remember (think about film themes, say Harry Potter or Star Wars).
  2. Don't stick to three or four notes in the same octave, don't hesitate to go higher or lower. But not too much, you don't want it to be eaten by another instrument's frequencies! Make it high enough in pitch so it is easy to hear and recognize.
  3. Have the chords playing in the background to guide you. You can reuse the notes of the chord in your melody too if you want to.
  4. Make use of silence for emphasis. Think about it as punctuation in a text, it can help structure your melodic sentences.
  5. As explained in a previous chapter, add a bit of surprise!

The bass

You know what? Writing the bass line is almost like writing a melody, except that:

  1. It can be much simpler, or be as complex.
  2. It emphasize much more the chords by playing a lot their fundamentals. For instance, you usually want to play a G on top of a G chord. It helps to solve ambiguous situations for the brain by telling it: "this is my fundamental, so please hear my chord like this". It does not always work, but it definitely has an impact on their interpretation.
  3. It is very low in pitch.
  4. It is much more subtle to hear, and depending of the mixing can be hard to hear on some devices. (The fix here is on the mixing side, where the bass needs some high frequencies as well to be heard on all devices. But that's for another chapter!)

Closing words

There is nothing fundamentally hard technically in writing a melody or a bass line. The hardest is making it sound good, because it is much more based on feelings than rules. Your brain also has to be compliant by giving you inspiration! (I hate when my best ideas pop up at work or in the metro!) At the end of the day, it is a lot of trial and error. Practice do help a lot. If it is really hard though, listening to other songs and taking inspiration by analyzing it can help a lot. I listened to a dozen of songs and improvised for a good thirty minutes before figuring out a melody I liked for this song.

Anyway, that's it for today! And remember to not put too much pressure on yourself, enjoying the process and having fun is the most important! Next time, we will learn how to make a fully fledged song by thinking about the different parts of it.

In the meantime, have a good day, afternoon, evening or night, and see you in the next post!
